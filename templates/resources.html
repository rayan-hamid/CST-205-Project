Looking for more information on Climate Change? Here are our most trusted sites.

The United Nations (UN) is an international organization founded in 1945, following the devastation of World War II, to promote global peace, security, and cooperation among nations. Comprising 193 member states, it addresses global challenges such as poverty, climate change, human rights, and health crises. The UN operates through specialized agencies like UNICEF, WHO, and UNESCO, and is guided by its core principles outlined in the UN Charter. Its headquarters is in New York City, serving as a hub for diplomacy and international collaboration. The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) is the United States' federal agency responsible for space exploration, scientific discovery, and aeronautics research. Established in 1958, NASA has pioneered advancements in space technology, sent humans to the Moon, and continues to explore the universe through missions like the Mars rovers and the James Webb Space Telescope. It also focuses on Earth sciences, studying climate change, and developing innovative technologies to benefit humanity. With its headquarters in Washington, D.C., NASA collaborates with international partners to push the boundaries of human knowledge and exploration. The World Health Organization (WHO) is a specialized agency of the United Nations responsible for global public health. Established in 1948 and headquartered in Geneva, Switzerland, it works to combat diseases, promote health equity, and coordinate international responses to health emergencies. The WHO develops global health standards, conducts research, and provides guidance on issues like infectious diseases, nutrition, and mental health. Key initiatives include eradicating polio, combating pandemics, and addressing the social determinants of health. Through collaboration with member states and partners, the WHO strives to ensure that everyone has access to the highest attainable standard of health.
